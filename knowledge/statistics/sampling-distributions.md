# Sampling Distributions

## Central question

How would an estimator vary if we repeatedly drew new samples from the same population and applied the same estimation rule?

## Short answer

The sampling distribution of an estimator is its probability distribution across all possible samples generated by the assumed sampling process.

## Intuition

A single dataset gives one estimate. Statistical inference asks how much that estimate could have changed if a different random sample had been observed.

Imagine repeatedly taking samples of size $n$, computing the estimator in every sample and recording the result. The distribution of those results is the sampling distribution.

This repeated-sampling experiment is conceptual. We usually observe only one actual sample, but probability theory describes the set of samples that could have occurred.

## Formal setup

Let

$$
\hat\theta_n=T(X_1,\ldots,X_n)
$$

be an estimator of a parameter $\theta$. The joint distribution of the random sample $(X_1,\ldots,X_n)$ induces a probability distribution for $\hat\theta_n$.

That induced distribution is the sampling distribution of $\hat\theta_n$.

## Example: sample mean

Suppose $X_1,\ldots,X_n$ are iid with

$$
E[X_i]=\mu
$$

and

$$
\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=\sigma^2.
$$

The sample mean is

$$
\bar X_n=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i.
$$

Its sampling mean is

$$
E[\bar X_n]=\mu
$$

and its sampling variance is

$$
\operatorname{Var}(\bar X_n)=\frac{\sigma^2}{n}.
$$

If the observations are normally distributed, the sampling distribution is exactly normal:

$$
\bar X_n\sim N\left(\mu,\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right).
$$

Without normal observations, the central limit theorem can still make this a useful large-sample approximation under suitable conditions.

## Mean, variance and shape

Three central features of a sampling distribution are:

1. its center, which is connected to bias;
2. its spread, which measures sampling uncertainty;
3. its shape, which determines how probabilities and critical values are calculated.

An estimator can have the correct center but high variance. It can also have low variance but be systematically centered away from the target.

## Exact versus asymptotic distributions

An exact or finite-sample distribution is valid for a specified sample size under the stated assumptions.

An asymptotic distribution describes the limiting behavior of a transformed estimator as

$$
n\rightarrow\infty.
$$

For example,

$$
\sqrt{n}(\hat\theta_n-\theta)
\overset{d}{\longrightarrow}
N(0,V)
$$

is an asymptotic statement. It motivates the approximation

$$
\hat\theta_n
\approx
N\left(\theta,\frac{V}{n}\right)
$$

for sufficiently large $n$.

The quality of this approximation depends on the data-generating process and sample size.

## Conditional and unconditional sampling distributions

In regression, a sampling distribution can be studied conditional on the observed regressor matrix $X$ or unconditionally over the joint randomness of $X$ and $Y$.

For example, under zero conditional mean,

$$
E[\hat\beta\mid X]=\beta.
$$

Taking another expectation gives

$$
E[\hat\beta]=E[E[\hat\beta\mid X]]=\beta.
$$

Conditional analysis treats the regressors as given after conditioning. Unconditional analysis averages over their possible samples as well.

## Standard deviation and standard error

The sampling standard deviation is

$$
\operatorname{SD}(\hat\theta_n)
=
\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(\hat\theta_n)}.
$$

Because it generally contains unknown population quantities, it is estimated from the sample. This estimate is the standard error:

$$
\operatorname{SE}(\hat\theta_n).
$$

A small standard error indicates that the estimator would vary relatively little across repeated samples under the model and assumptions.

## Sampling distribution of OLS

The OLS estimator depends on every observation in the sample. Therefore, different samples produce different values of $\hat\beta$.

Under least-squares assumptions, the course studies:

- the conditional and unconditional mean of $\hat\beta$;
- its conditional variance;
- its complicated exact distribution under general non-normal errors;
- its approximately normal large-sample distribution.

These properties make hypothesis tests and confidence intervals possible.

## Estimator distribution versus data distribution

The distribution of the raw observations is not the same as the sampling distribution of an estimator.

For example, individual incomes may be strongly right-skewed, while the sampling distribution of the average income may be approximately normal in a sufficiently large sample.

## Simulation interpretation

A simulation can approximate a sampling distribution:

1. choose a data-generating process;
2. draw a sample;
3. calculate the estimator;
4. repeat many times;
5. plot the resulting estimates.

The simulation does not prove a theorem, but it can reveal bias, variance, approximation quality and the effect of sample size.

## Common mistakes

- Calling the distribution of observations the sampling distribution.
- Thinking the sampling distribution is obtained from variation within one sample.
- Treating an asymptotic approximation as exact.
- Confusing the standard error of an estimate with the standard deviation of the data.
- Forgetting that a sampling distribution depends on the assumed data-generating and sampling process.
- Mixing conditional and unconditional results without stating which one is used.
